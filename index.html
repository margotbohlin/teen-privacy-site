Who Is Our Audience?

Teenagers today are among the most active social media users, often engaging with platforms that encourage frequent sharing of personal details. This openness is influenced not only by the features of emerging apps but also by shifting cultural norms around visibility and self-expression online. As teens adopt new technologies, they tend to share more without fully understanding the long-term implications of their digital footprints [3]. Many privacy laws don't fully protect users over the age of 13, and because teens are so active online, advertisers and tech companies see them as prime targets. Our goal is to raise awareness, explain the risks, and share a few ways to take back control of your online privacy.

Where is Your Data Going?

Once teens start using social media, their personal information is on full display. Every like, comment, photo upload, and search contributes to a growing digital profile. This data becomes vulnerable to misuse not only by the platform itself but also by third-party advertisers. One key concern is data harvesting, which can be described as the large-scale collection and analysis of user information, often without explicit consent. This can include personal details like age, location, interests, habits, device info, and browsing history. These profiles are then used to build sophisticated targeting algorithms, allowing companies to tailor ads that are eerily specific, sometimes manipulating user behavior or emotions. Teens, in particular, may not realize that what feels like a harmless or entertaining experience is actually feeding an invisible surveillance infrastructure. Their data can be used not just for selling products, but also for influencing political views, shaping online experiences, and feeding into broader databases accessed by the government, advertising agencies, market research firms, and of course, the social media platform itself, such as Facebook [1].

Capitalistic Gain

Facebook is a great example of a platform that has turned user activity and personal data into a powerful engine for advertising revenue. Every interaction is tracked and analyzed, not just to improve the user experience, but to maximize profit through hyper-targeted ads. Companies like Facebook collect vast amounts of user information, often under the guise of improving services, using privacy policies and terms of service that are intentionally dense, vague, or long-winded. These documents are written in a way that makes it unlikely the average user, especially a teenager, fully understands what they’re consenting to. As a result, people routinely agree to surveillance they don’t understand. Users essentially are becoming unpaid laborers contributing to corporate profits by providing data, content, and engagement [1]. The next time you use social media to seek travel advice or explore a destination, pay close attention to how quickly you're shown ads for airlines, local tours, or travel insurance. This likely is not coincidence, it is a demonstration of surveillance capitalism in action.

Harm: Doxxing & Exploitation

Monetary gain is not the only objective for social media platforms. Beyond targeted advertising and corporate profit, these platforms facilitate the vast circulation of personal information, muchof which users share without fully considering the consequences. Details such as age, gender, location, photos, and interests may seem harmless in isolation, but when combined, they form a comprehensive digital footprint that can be exploited in unexpected and dangerous ways [5]. In some online communities, this personal data becomes a weapon. Doxxing is the act of publicly revealing private or identifying information about someone, and has become an increasingly common tactic used to intimidate, harass, or silence individuals [6]. This can include anything from publishing a home address to leaking private messages or photos, often with malicious intent. While anyone can be a target, teens and other vulnerable groups are especially at risk, as they may be less equipped to recognize red flags or respond effectively when their privacy is breached.

How to Protect Your Privacy

  • Data Education: Teens especially need to be taught early on to manage their digital presence. Schools and libraries should offer programs that teach students how to read privacy policies, use privacy settings effectively, and recognize suspicious links or phishing attempts. These programs should be integrated into digital literacy curriculums and updated regularly to reflect the rapidly evolving tech landscape [2]. At UNC we see these attacks regularly through spam emails coming from sources outside of the organization.
  • Research Guidelines: Beyond individual users, institutions also play a key role in protecting personal data. Researchers and social media legal teams must develop clear guidelines for handling networked data that contains personally identifiable information (addresses, security questions, birthdays, etc.). As more research involves social media platforms and online behaviors, it's essential to have shared ethical standards and transparent procedures for data collection, consent, and storage [4].
  • Ad-Blockers/Further Technology Development: Developers and engineers can help shift the balance from harm to good by creating apps and browser extensions that limit tracking or alert users when their data is being collected. One example could be a browser plugin that flags when a site is sharing your location or a "privacy score" tool that rates apps before you download them. This would be a lot easier for teens to comprehend than having to read the fine print in platform terms and conditions.
